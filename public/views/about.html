Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi, whose full name is Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, was a prominent leader in India's struggle for independence from British rule. He is known for his philosophy of nonviolent resistance, which he called "Satyagraha," and his role in inspiring and leading various civil disobedience movements to achieve social and political change. Here's an overview of his life and contributions:

Gandhi was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, a town in present-day Gujarat, India. He came from a middle-class family and was raised with traditional Indian values. He received his education in India and then went to study law in London, where he was exposed to Western culture and political ideas.

After completing his studies in London, Gandhi moved to South Africa to work as a lawyer. During his time there, he became acutely aware of the racial discrimination and injustice faced by Indians under British colonial rule. These experiences marked a turning point in his life and inspired his commitment to social and political activism.

Gandhi's leadership played a crucial role in India's struggle for independence from British rule. His advocacy for nonviolent resistance gained widespread support, and his ability to mobilize large sections of the Indian population made him a unifying figure in the fight for freedom.
